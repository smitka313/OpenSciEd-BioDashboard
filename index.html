Coursewide Literacy Threads

QuestioningStudents repeatedly generate, refine, categorize, and revisit questions through Driving Question Board routines.
Data & RepresentationStudents interpret graphs, tables, maps, simulations, images, readings, and models, then compare evidence across representations.
ModelingStudents build, critique, revise, and transfer models as new evidence becomes available.
ArgumentationStudents make claims, select evidence, explain reasoning, compare competing explanations, and justify recommendations.
Decision-MakingStudents evaluate solutions using criteria, constraints, trade-offs, stakeholder needs, and scientific evidence.

What It Sounds Like

  • “What evidence supports your claim?”
  • “Is that a pattern, a cause, or both?”
  • “How does your model explain that?”
  • “What happens if this variable changes?”
  • “Are we talking about an individual, population, ecosystem, or system?”

Literacy Progression

  • B.1: Ask questions, build models, and explain ecosystem patterns.
  • B.2: Track matter and energy using lab evidence and systems models.
  • B.3: Explain mechanisms from DNA to cells to body systems.
  • B.4: Use data to explain population change over generations.
  • B.5: Argue from evidence about ancestry, speciation, extinction, and solutions.

Teacher Move → Literacy Move

  • “What is your evidence?” → Argumentation
  • “Show that in your model.” → Modeling
  • “What pattern do you notice?” → Data interpretation
  • “What scale are we using?” → Scale literacy
  • “What trade-off matters most?” → Decision literacy

Pacing & Streamlining Guide

When time is tight, protect the storyline.

Keep the anchoring phenomenon, at least one core investigation per lesson set, consensus model / Putting Pieces Together lessons, and the final transfer or decision task. Streamline by combining readings, reducing repeated practice, or using teacher-provided data — not by removing the sensemaking arc.

Unit 1: Ecosystems

Must Keep

  • Lesson 1 phenomenon + DQB
  • At least one full ecosystem data investigation
  • Model revision / synthesis lesson
  • Final ecosystem explanation or transfer

Can Streamline

  • Combine multiple data days into one structured analysis day
  • Use quick-write + share instead of extended discussion
  • Focus on fewer case studies

Trim Carefully

  • Repeated modeling cycles, while keeping one strong revision
  • Extended discussion time after sensemaking is secure

Unit 2: Matter & Energy

Must Keep

  • Zombie fire / peat phenomenon
  • At least one hands-on investigation
  • Carbon cycle and feedback modeling
  • Fire management decision-making

Can Streamline

  • Turn some readings into jigsaws
  • Use a demo instead of one full lab
  • Shorten model-building iterations

Trim Carefully

  • Secondary carbon sink case studies
  • Extra datasets, while keeping one strong example

Unit 3: Inheritance

Must Keep

  • Who gets cancer anchoring phenomenon
  • Cell cycle + p53 modeling
  • DNA → protein → trait explanation
  • Genetic + environmental risk synthesis

Can Streamline

  • Compress simulations into guided analysis
  • Combine inheritance and mutation review
  • Use guided notes for selected readings

Trim Carefully

  • Extended pedigree practice
  • Extra case comparisons, while keeping at least one

Unit 4: Natural Selection

Must Keep

  • At least one full natural selection case investigation
  • Population-level explanation over generations
  • Genetic diversity / fragmentation lesson
  • Urban design solution task

Can Streamline

  • Combine species case studies
  • Use one strong dataset instead of several
  • Shorten repeated graphing practice

Trim Carefully

  • Intermediate modeling steps
  • Multiple practice datasets after students show understanding

Unit 5: Speciation

Must Keep

  • Arctic bear phenomenon
  • Evolutionary tree / common ancestry work
  • Speciation argument
  • Conservation decision or transfer task

Can Streamline

  • Merge evidence sources into one synthesis task
  • Use teacher-provided data for one analysis
  • Shorten extinction comparison activities

Trim Carefully

  • Some extinction case studies
  • Extended discussion cycles, while preserving argument writing

How Biology Connects to Other OpenSciEd Courses

Cross-Course Coherence

The Biology units are part of a larger OpenSciEd high school sequence. These connections help teachers see how Biology ideas prepare students for Chemistry, Physics, and Earth & Space Science work.

  • Use in PLCs: identify where ideas continue across courses.
  • Use for planning: preview concepts students may revisit later.
  • Use with students: show why Biology connects beyond one course.

B.1

Ecosystem Interactions
  • Connects to Earth systems and environmental change.
  • Builds systems thinking, interactions, and stability/change reasoning.
  • Prepares students to model complex systems in later science courses.

B.2

Matter & Energy
  • Connects strongly to Chemistry through matter, energy, carbon, and reactions.
  • Prepares students for thermodynamics and chemical reaction reasoning.
  • Reinforces the distinction: matter cycles, energy flows.

B.3

Inheritance & Variation
  • Connects to Chemistry through molecular structure, proteins, and DNA.
  • Builds structure-function reasoning from molecules to body systems.
  • Prepares students to explain mechanisms across scales.

B.4

Natural Selection
  • Connects to Earth systems through environmental change and urbanization.
  • Builds population-level reasoning and data-based explanation.
  • Prepares students to evaluate human impacts on ecosystems.

B.5

Common Ancestry & Speciation
  • Connects to Earth history, climate change, and extinction patterns.
  • Builds argumentation from fossil, DNA, climate, and model evidence.
  • Prepares students for system-level reasoning about biodiversity loss.
